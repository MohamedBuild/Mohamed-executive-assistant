# LinkedIn Post Templates

## How to use
Pick a type. Pick a template. Copy the example as a starting point or use the structure to write from scratch. Batch your week on Sunday — aim for variety across types.

**Structure legend:**
- **Hook formula** — what the first line does (people see 2-3 lines before "see more")
- **Body structure** — how to build the middle
- **Close** — how to end it
- **Example** — filled-in version ready to adapt

**LinkedIn mechanics this library is built around:**
- First line wins or loses the post — make it a statement or a contradiction, never a preamble
- Short lines. One idea per line. White space is readable.
- No "In today's fast-paced world." No "I'm thrilled to share." No throat-clearing.
- End with a question or a quiet challenge — not a hard sell
- No hashtag walls. 2-3 max if you use any.

# Thought Leadership

*Your take on trends, mistakes, and things people won't say out loud.*

---

## TL-1: The Contrarian Take

**Hook formula:** State the common belief. Immediately contradict it.

**Body structure:**
- Line 1–2: the common belief + your counter
- Explain why the common belief exists (one line)
- Your argument: 3–4 short punchy lines
- Land the point — the implication

**Close:** A direct question to the reader

**Example:**
```
Most people think automation is about saving time.

It's not. It's about removing decisions.

Time is easy to find. Decision fatigue is what kills small business owners — the 40 micro-calls a day that drain everything before noon.

The best automations don't speed things up. They remove the moment where you have to decide at all.

That's what I build. Not faster wheels. No steering wheel.

What decision would you most want to remove from your day?
```

---

## TL-2: The Mistake I See

**Hook formula:** "The biggest mistake [audience] make with [topic] isn't [obvious thing]. It's [unexpected thing]."

**Body structure:**
- Hook
- Name the common assumption (one line)
- The real problem underneath it
- What it costs them if they don't fix it
- What to do instead — brief, no lecture

**Close:** Observation or open question

**Example:**
```
The biggest mistake SME owners make with automation isn't picking the wrong tool.

It's automating a broken process.

They take something that's already inefficient, add a workflow on top, and wonder why it still doesn't work.

Automation amplifies what's already there. A messy process becomes a faster mess.

Fix the process first. Automate second.

I've turned down projects because of this. It's the right call every time.

What's a process you automated that needed fixing first?
```

---

## TL-3: The Unpopular Truth

**Hook formula:** A bold claim that most people in your space won't say publicly.

**Body structure:**
- The claim — no hedging
- Why most people avoid saying this
- Evidence or reasoning — 2–3 short paragraphs
- Acknowledge the counterargument in one line

**Close:** Challenge the reader or invite pushback

**Example:**
```
Most AI tools being sold to SMEs are solving problems that don't exist.

Founders buy them because it feels productive. Not because they're fixing anything real.

I audit client stacks regularly. Five tools, half of them barely used, all generating noise instead of output.

Yes, there are exceptions. But they're rare. Most "automation" I see is complexity wearing a productivity badge.

The boring truth: a well-structured spreadsheet beats a badly configured AI workflow every time.

What tool have you used that was more hassle than it was worth?
```

# Case Studies

*Client wins. Problem → process → result. Keep it specific.*

---

## CS-1: Problem → Process → Result

**Hook formula:** "[Client type] came to me with [problem]. Here's what we built."

**Body structure:**
- Hook: who + what problem
- Situation in 2–3 lines — make it feel real
- What you built: 3–4 bullet points (use →)
- The result — specific number or before/after

**Close:** The broader lesson this illustrates

**Example:**
```
An HR consultant came to me drowning in client onboarding.

Every new client meant 4 hours of manual work — collecting documents, chasing signatures, updating spreadsheets, sending the same emails by hand. She had the clients. She didn't have the capacity.

Here's what we built:

→ Automated intake form connected to her CRM
→ Document collection with reminders built in
→ Welcome pack sent automatically on form submission
→ Live status dashboard — no chasing required

Onboarding time: 4 hours → 20 minutes.

The work didn't disappear. It just stopped needing her in the room.
```

---

## CS-2: The Before / After

**Hook formula:** "Before: [painful state]. After: [transformed state]."

**Body structure:**
- Before: 2–3 lines, specific and relatable
- After: 2–3 lines, the contrast
- What changed — the actual mechanism (one paragraph)
- Time/result metric

**Close:** The one-line lesson

**Example:**
```
Before: a consultant spending Sunday evenings manually preparing client reports.

After: reports generated and delivered automatically every Friday at 5pm.

What changed: one workflow connecting their project tool, a Google Sheet, and an email trigger.

Built in a day. Running for 6 months without touching it.

The best automation is the one you forget exists.
```

---

## CS-3: The Specific Number

**Hook formula:** Lead with a result metric. Make it concrete.

**Body structure:**
- The number (hook — first line)
- Context: who, what was the problem
- The solution — brief, no jargon
- How the number was reached

**Close:** The broader implication for the reader

**Example:**
```
3 hours saved per client per week.

That was the result for an agency owner managing his entire pipeline from his inbox.

The fix wasn't complex. A CRM workflow connected to Gmail — auto-tagging, follow-up reminders, a weekly summary report.

No new software. Just the tools he already had, connected properly.

3 hours a week is 150 hours a year. At his day rate, that's a significant number to leave on the table.

Most automation wins aren't from new tools. They're from connecting what you already have.
```

# Tips / How-To

*Practical, saveable advice. One clear idea per post.*

---

## TIP-1: The Numbered List

**Hook formula:** "[Number] [things/signs/rules] for [topic]." Keep it specific — not generic.

**Body structure:**
- Hook line
- The list — each point on its own line, one sentence
- Brief expansion on the most important point (optional)

**Close:** Question that makes the reader apply it to themselves

**Example:**
```
5 signs a process is ready to automate:

1. You do it the same way every time
2. It involves moving data from one place to another
3. You've trained someone else to do it exactly as you do
4. The biggest risk is forgetting to do it at all
5. You dread it every time it comes up

If 3 or more apply, it's automatable.

Which one hits hardest for you?
```

---

## TIP-2: The Single Insight

**Hook formula:** A counterintuitive statement about one narrow thing.

**Body structure:**
- The insight (hook)
- Why most people miss it or do the opposite
- Build the explanation — 3–4 short paragraphs, step by step
- Practical takeaway — what to actually do

**Close:** Invite the reader to reflect on their own situation

**Example:**
```
The best time to document a process is while you're doing it for the first time.

Most people document after. By then they've forgotten the decisions they made along the way. What comes out is a summary, not a process.

Document as you go. Screenshot, voice note, quick bullet — anything. Clean it up later.

That raw documentation becomes the foundation for everything else: training, delegation, automation.

If you can't document it, you can't hand it off. And if you can't hand it off, you own it forever.

What process have you been meaning to document for months?
```

---

## TIP-3: The Comparison / Framework

**Hook formula:** "X vs Y — here's when each one is the right call."

**Body structure:**
- Name the comparison
- When X makes sense — one line
- When Y makes sense — one line
- The decision rule you actually use

**Close:** Ask which situation the reader is in right now

**Example:**
```
Build vs buy.

Most founders default to buy — a new tool for every problem. Fast, low friction, adds up.

Buy when: proven problem, standard use case, budget is there.
Build when: unique workflow, tools don't connect, you've already bought three things that don't quite fit.

The rule I use: if you can describe the output in one sentence, it's probably buildable. If you can't, buy first and learn from it.

Which camp are you in right now?
```

# Behind the Scenes

*How you work, what you've learned, the reality behind the polished output.*

---

## BTS-1: Real Project Lesson

**Hook formula:** Something happened on a project that changed how you work. Don't say "I'm proud of" — show what went wrong or what surprised you.

**Body structure:**
- What the project was (no client names)
- What you expected to happen
- What actually happened
- The lesson — stated plainly

**Close:** How you've changed your approach since

**Example:**
```
I built a client a full onboarding workflow in two days.

They never used it.

Not because it didn't work. Because their team didn't know it existed. I handed over a finished product and assumed they'd figure it out.

The lesson: the handover is half the project.

Now I build a walkthrough session into every engagement. Documentation. A short video if it's complex. Nothing is "done" until the client is running it themselves.

A workflow no one uses is just a file on a server.

What's the most important part of a project you've learned not to skip?
```

---

## BTS-2: How I Work

**Hook formula:** "My actual process for [task] — no polish, just what I do."

**Body structure:**
- Name the task/challenge
- The real process — numbered steps, short lines
- What you tried that didn't work
- What stuck and why

**Close:** Invite others to share their own approach

**Example:**
```
My actual process for scoping a new client project:

1. Start with the output — what does "done" look like exactly
2. Work backwards to find every step that has to happen
3. Mark which steps are currently manual and painful
4. Decide which ones to automate first
5. Reality-check the timeline against actual dependencies

Step 1 is where most scope creep starts. Clients know they want it faster or easier — they don't always know what they want at the end.

The first 20 minutes of every discovery call is me doing step 1 out loud with them.

How do you scope new projects?
```

---

## BTS-3: Tools / Stack

**Hook formula:** "The tools I actually run [business/task] on — and why."

**Body structure:**
- Brief framing (not a list for list's sake)
- 4–5 tools, one line each with the actual reason
- What you've cut and why
- The principle behind your choices

**Close:** Ask what they can't run their business without

**Example:**
```
The tools I run AGENCINA on. No affiliate links.

→ Claude — thinking partner, first draft of everything, code review
→ Google Workspace — docs, sheets, calendar. Still unbeatable for collaboration.
→ n8n — workflow automation, self-hosted, nothing I can't customise
→ Notion — client briefs and project tracking
→ Gmail — best email client if you keep it simple

What I've cut: anything that requires another tool to manage it.

My rule: if a new tool doesn't replace something or remove a decision, it doesn't get added. The stack should shrink over time, not grow.

What's the one tool you couldn't run your business without?
```

# Engagement Questions

*Short posts designed to start a conversation. Hook → your take → open it up.*

---

## EQ-1: The Opinion Poll

**Hook formula:** "[Topic] — [Option A] or [Option B]? I'll share which I'd pick."

**Body structure:**
- Name the choice clearly
- One line for each side — acknowledge both fairly
- Your pick + one-line reason (don't sit on the fence)

**Close:** Ask for their vote and the reason behind it

**Example:**
```
Specialism vs generalism — which builds a stronger agency?

Specialism: easier to sell, higher rates, deeper expertise.
Generalism: more resilience, broader client base, never bored.

I'm picking specialism. Depth beats breadth when you're competing on quality, not price.

But I've seen both work. What's your take — and why?
```

---

## EQ-2: The Experience Question

**Hook formula:** "What's the [most underrated / worst / best] [thing] you've dealt with in [context]?"

**Body structure:**
- The question (hook)
- One line of context — why you're asking
- Your own answer — short, specific, honest

**Close:** Invite their response

**Example:**
```
What's the most underrated automation for a small service business?

I ask because the ones that get talked about most — lead gen, chatbots, content — are rarely the ones that save the most time.

Mine: automated invoice reminders. Boring, runs in the background, never gets celebrated. Saves more awkward conversations than anything else I've built.

What's yours?
```

---

## EQ-3: The Prediction

**Hook formula:** "My prediction for [topic] by [timeframe]. Agree or disagree?"

**Body structure:**
- The prediction — specific, not vague
- Brief reasoning: 2–3 lines
- Acknowledge the main counterargument honestly

**Close:** Ask if they agree and what they're seeing

**Example:**
```
My prediction: by 2027, most SME owners in MENA will have at least one AI-powered workflow running in their business — whether they built it or bought it.

The tools are already simple enough. The gap now is knowing what to automate, not how.

The counterargument: adoption is slower than anyone predicts. Fair. But the cost of not adopting will show up as competitive gaps before most people notice.

Agree or disagree? What are you seeing in your market?
```
